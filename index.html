History of
 Japanese Fashion 

1860s-　　　　　　1910s-　　　　　　1960s-　　　　　　1980s　　　　　1990s　　　　　　2000s　　　　　　2017s










1860s-1910s

At this time, "kimono," which translates to "thing worn," is used to describe a traditional Japanese T-shaped robe.
Kimonos are placed left side over right side. A haori is a jacket traditionally worn over a kimono.
Shibori was a popular technique used on both kimonos and haoris. It involved stitching then hand-dying fabric to create unique patterns.










1910s-1950s

Industrial developments made silk kimonos less expensive and more accessible.
Art Deco and Art Nouveau inspired designs were on trend. This kimono is hand-painted on silk panels.




Though Western-style clothes grew more popular at this time, kimonos were still worn during traditional events.










1960s-1970s

By the 1960s, Japanese prints were appearing on Western-style dresses.


At the same time, kimonos were often machine-printed instead of hand-painted or dyed.










1980s

Avant-garde fashion gained popularity in Japan.
Designers like Rei Kawakubo, COMME des GARCONS, Yohji Yamamoto and Issey Miyake made unstructured designs with asymmetric silhouettes.










1990s

Styles worn in Harajuku -a neighborhood in Tokyo- inspired a number of subcultures.
Subcultures included: Lolita, Goth Lolita, Kogal, Decora, Fairy Kei, and more.
Goth Lolita, which was Victorian-inspired, featured dark colors, petticoats, lace, and ruffles.










2000s

Fairy Kei was inspired by child pop culture references - My Little Pony, Strawberry Shortcake, and Care Bears.
It featured pastel colors including colored wigs with tons of clips and bows.
Fairy Kei exeamplifies the still popular Japanese "kawaii" aesthetic, meaning "cuteness."










2017s

In 2017, the trend was oversized bottoms paired with a pop of color.
Both are subtle tributes to the evolution of Japanese fashion over the decades.
